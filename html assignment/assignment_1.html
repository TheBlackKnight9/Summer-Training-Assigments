Q1.What is the significance of the South China Sea in geopolitics

Ans.The South China Sea is crucial due to its strategic shipping lanes, abundant fishing grounds, and potential oil and gas reserves. Several countries, including China, Vietnam, and the Philippines, have overlapping territorial claims. China's militarization of artificial islands has heightened regional tensions and drawn international attention.


Q2.Why is the Russia-Ukraine conflict geopolitically important?

Ans.The war has reshaped global alliances and highlighted the fragility of European security. It triggered Western sanctions against Russia, increased NATO unity, and created an energy crisis by disrupting oil and gas flows. The conflict also challenged norms of sovereignty and territorial integrity.


Q3.How does the U.S.-China rivalry shape global geopolitics?

Ans.The U.S.-China rivalry spans trade, technology, military power, and influence in international institutions. It's driving a shift toward a multipolar world and creating geopolitical fault lines, especially in the Indo-Pacific region. Countries are being pressured to align with either power or maintain neutrality.


Q4.What is BRICS and why is it geopolitically significant?

Ans.BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) represents a bloc of emerging economies challenging Western dominance. It aims to reform global financial institutions and promote multipolarity. Its expansion and coordination on development issues influence global economic and political agendas.


Q5. How does the Arctic region influence future geopolitics?

Ans.Melting ice is opening new shipping routes and access to untapped resources like oil and gas. This has sparked territorial claims and interest from countries like Russia, the U.S., and China. The Arctic could become a new frontier of strategic competition and cooperation.


Q6. Why is the Middle East geopolitically volatile?

Ans.The Middle East is a hotspot due to ethnic and religious conflicts, authoritarian regimes, and vast oil reserves. External powers often intervene for strategic and economic reasons. Ongoing issues like the Israel-Palestine conflict and Iran's nuclear program fuel regional instability.
